## You pay taxes. You deserve to know where the money goes.

Every year, your town collects property taxes, sales taxes, fees — and spends them. On roads, schools, police, parks, contracts, salaries. That spending is public record. It's your money, and the law says you have a right to see how it's used.

But there's a catch. "Public record" usually means a 200-page PDF buried on a government website, written in accounting language, with no context and no one to explain it. Most people never see it. Most people don't know it exists.

A third of American counties have no local newspaper anymore. The reporters who used to sit through every city council meeting, read every budget document, and ask why the parks department line item doubled — they're gone. Closing at more than two papers per week for the past decade.

Budget Watch fills that gap. We read the budgets, flag what deserves a question, and publish it in plain language — so taxpayers can hold their towns accountable.

## What we do

We obtain official published budget documents for small towns — especially those without a local newspaper — and run a systematic analysis. We compare spending year over year, compare per-capita figures against peer towns of similar size and region, and flag lines that deviate from what you'd expect.

Every flag becomes a **question to raise** — not an accusation. A budget anomaly almost always has an innocent explanation: a one-time capital purchase, a grant that didn't renew, a departmental reorganization. Our job isn't to find wrongdoing. It's to make sure someone asks.

## What we don't do

We don't call anything corruption. We don't call anyone corrupt. We don't use the word "audit" — we're analysts, not auditors. We don't invent or estimate figures; every number in our reports is cited to a specific page of the official document. We don't editorialize. We let the numbers speak and let the public ask.

## The methodology

Our five checks look at:

1. **Year-over-year spikes** — categories that grew more than 20% in a single year
2. **Peer-spending outliers** — per-capita figures more than 50% above comparable towns
3. **Fund balance erosion** — reserves declining two years in a row
4. **Reserves vs. service cuts** — towns holding large reserves while cutting public services
5. **Unexplained transfers** — inter-fund movements above a threshold without a clear documented reason

Every threshold is editorial judgment, and every threshold is published. You can read the exact logic in `analyzer.py` in our public repository. We hold ourselves to the same transparency we ask of others.

## Who's behind this

Budget Watch is an independent project. It has no political affiliation and accepts no funding from government sources or from organizations with interests in the towns we cover.

## How to help

- **Submit a town.** If you live in or near a news-desert town and can point us to a published budget document, [let us know](/submit/).
- **Challenge our analysis.** If you think we got something wrong — a misread figure, a wrong peer town, a flag that has an obvious explanation we missed — email us. We'll correct the record publicly.
- **Spread the word.** Share reports with local community groups, libraries, school boards, or anyone who pays taxes in the towns we cover.
