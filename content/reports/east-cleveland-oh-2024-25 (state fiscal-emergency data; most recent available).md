## I. Executive Summary

East Cleveland, one of Ohio’s poorest cities, has spent more than 30 years in state fiscal emergency — continuously since 2012, after an earlier 1988–2006 stint. In September 2025 the Ohio Auditor moved to place the city under a court-appointed receiver, a first under new state law, citing a structural general-fund deficit and at least $65 million in unpaid legal judgments. The move followed the corruption conviction of the sitting mayor and a contested leadership succession.

**Total Budget:** ~$12 million (General Fund expenditures, last fiscal year, per state) &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Per Capita:** ~$895 (GF spending ~$12M / pop. 13,402) &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Fund Balance:** Structural deficit: ~$11M GF revenue vs ~$12M spending; deficits in 10 budgeted funds; began the year with >$1M in unpaid bills &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Debt Load:** At least $65 million in legal judgments (per Ohio Auditor)

## VII. Governance & Transparency Audit

Oversight has run through a state Financial Planning and Supervision Commission since 2012, yet the city never exited emergency. Mayor Brandon King was indicted and convicted on corruption charges and lost office; interim Mayor Sandra Morgan was replaced by then-Council President Lateek Shabazz after a court battle, with a Nov. 4, 2025 election to follow. In Sept. 2025 the Auditor moved for receivership under new ORC 118.29; the Attorney General is to ask the Ohio Court of Claims to appoint a receiver.

**Budget document publicly available:** Partial — finances are documented by the Ohio Auditor and the state fiscal commission, but a current, accessible city budget was not found online; the receiver has questioned the accuracy of the city’s financial data. &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Last audit published:** City is audited by the Ohio Auditor of State; most recent city ACFR not located online for this review. Fiscal-emergency data current as of the Sept. 2025 receivership filing. &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Public hearings held:** Financial Planning and Supervision Commission meetings; City Council meetings (covered by Cleveland Documenters).

## VIII. Policy Options & Strategic Recommendations

1) Complete and publish an accurate, current ACFR and monthly financial reports so the receiver and public work from reliable numbers. 2) Negotiate and structure repayment of the $65M+ judgments. 3) Close the structural GF gap via shared services with Cuyahoga County/neighboring suburbs and right-sized staffing. 4) Pursue targeted revenue (collections, land-bank redevelopment) rather than across-the-board cuts. 5) Set clear, time-bound exit criteria from fiscal emergency.

## IX. Citizen Action Guide

1) Attend Financial Planning and Supervision Commission and City Council meetings; read Cleveland Documenters notes. 2) File Ohio public-records requests with the city and the Auditor for the latest audit and judgment list. 3) Track the Ohio Court of Claims receivership case and the receiver’s reports.

## X. Conclusion & Long-Term Outlook

Without decisive action under receivership, East Cleveland faces a fourth-plus decade of insolvency, continued population loss, and possible municipal bankruptcy. The 2025 receivership — the first under Ohio’s new law — is the most serious intervention yet; whether it produces a durable exit or merely another chapter depends on accurate books, judgment repayment, and a balanced budget over the next 3–5 years.
