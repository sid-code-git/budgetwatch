## I. Executive Summary

Gallup — the commercial hub for the Navajo Nation and Zuni Pueblo — closed FY2025 (year ended 6/30/2025) in strong shape on paper: its governmental funds held a combined fund balance of $80,211,945, and the General Fund alone carried an unassigned balance of $34,815,027, which rose $370,488 during the year. Yet the city it serves is deeply poor: 26.8% of residents live below the poverty line and median household income is $58,303. Gallup's economy — and its budget — rests heavily on Gross Receipts Tax (New Mexico's sales tax) generated by tens of thousands of tribal-area shoppers, and $17.29M of its $30.73M bonded debt is pledged against that GRT stream. The central accountability questions are concentration risk (what happens to GRT in a downturn or if retail shifts) and equity (how large reserves are deployed for a community with such high need).

## II. Socioeconomic Context & Peer Comparison

Gallup (pop. 21,023; Census Reporter ACS 2024 5-yr) is the seat of McKinley County in northwest New Mexico and the retail and services hub for the eastern Navajo Nation and Zuni Pueblo — an estimated 30,000-50,000 people from surrounding tribal areas shop in Gallup monthly, so its daytime/economic population far exceeds its residents. It is a low-income community: median household income $58,303 (about 90% of the NM median), per capita income just $25,302, and a 26.8% poverty rate (roughly 1.5x the state rate). Median home value is $165,200. McKinley County overall is poorer still (33.2% poverty). The city's finances are unusually strong relative to this need because it owns its electric, gas, water and wastewater utilities and collects heavy Gross Receipts Tax on regional retail.

### Population & Economic Trends

The city's population is roughly flat-to-declining (~21,000-21,900 over the last several years) while the surrounding tribal-area customer base keeps its commercial tax base large. Peer comparison — New Mexico regional hubs of similar size and role: Farmington (~44,000, larger San Juan County hub), Las Vegas NM (~12,500, San Miguel County seat), and Española (~10,000, Rio Arriba hub). Gallup's GRT-per-resident is far above these peers because so much of its retail is bought by non-residents.

## III. Revenue & Expenditure Ledger

### Where the Money Comes From

Gallup's dominant own-source revenue is Gross Receipts Tax (New Mexico's sales-type tax), driven by regional retail purchases from the Navajo Nation and Zuni Pueblo; the city layers a local option GRT on top of the state rate. It also runs large enterprise (utility) funds — Electric, Water, Wastewater, Solid Waste, plus a Surcharge and an Infrastructure GRT fund. Exact FY2025 revenue-by-source totals are itemized in the ACFR but are not cleanly extractable from the posted scanned PDF; the MD&A notes special-revenue growth (fire and law enforcement protection funds) and that the city had spent roughly half its ARPA allocation, leaving ~$1.25M in FY23. (Source: FY2025 ACFR; NM LFC Local Government Dashboard, May 2024.)

### Where the Money Goes

As a full-service home-rule city that owns its utilities, Gallup's spending spans general government, public safety (police and fire, with dedicated state law-enforcement and fire-protection funds), streets, culture/recreation, and large utility operations and capital. Function-level dollar detail is in the FY2025 ACFR; it is not cleanly extractable from the posted scanned document. A notable FY2025 policy item: the council weighed an Automated Speed Enforcement (camera) program routing fine revenue to the Police Department. (Source: FY2025 ACFR; Gallup City Council agenda, 6/27/2025.)

Note the debt structure: 56% of bonded debt ($17.29M) is repaid from Gross Receipts Tax and 41% ($12.65M) from utility revenues, so debt service is concentrated on GRT and utility income rather than the General Fund/property tax.

Gallup's $30.73M of bonded debt is modest against an $80.2M combined governmental fund balance, but its structure concentrates risk: more than half is pledged against Gross Receipts Tax, the same regional-retail revenue stream that funds operations. A sustained drop in tribal-area retail spending would pressure both the operating budget and GRT-backed debt service simultaneously. Utility-backed debt ($12.65M) depends on rate revenue from the city's electric/water/wastewater systems. Only $795,000 is property-tax-backed, reflecting New Mexico's GRT-centric municipal finance model.

## V. Community Impact Analysis

### A. Education & Youth Programs

Schools are run by Gallup-McKinley County Schools (a separate entity); the city's role is indirect. Municipal youth impact runs through parks, recreation, the library, and dedicated public-safety funds. The city's large reserves raise a fair public question about investment in youth and recreation amenities.

### B. Facilities, Infrastructure & Parks

Gallup owns and reinvests in its electric, water, wastewater and solid-waste utilities plus an 'Infrastructure GRT' fund dedicated to capital. This municipal-utility model is the reason a low-income city can sustain large reserves and a capital program. Water security in a high-desert border town remains a long-run infrastructure concern.

### C. Public Safety Staffing & Allocation

Public safety is a budget priority, with dedicated Law Enforcement Protection and Fire Protection special-revenue funds and new state funding for law-enforcement recruitment beginning FY23. In FY2025 the council considered an Automated Speed Enforcement (camera) ordinance (max $100 fines) with revenue directed to the Police Department — a measure to watch for both safety impact and revenue/equity effects.

### D. Social Services & Senior Programs

Gallup is a regional social-services and health hub, including detox/behavioral-health services (e.g., the Na'Nizhoozhi Center) tied to the area's well-documented alcohol-related public-health burden. With 26.8% of residents in poverty and a much larger low-income customer/visitor base, demand for social services is high relative to the resident tax base — sharpening the question of how the city's large reserves are used.

## VII. Governance & Transparency Audit

Gallup is a home-rule municipality (founded 1881) with a mayor-council form; FY2025 leadership was Mayor Louie Bonaguidi and City Manager Jon DeYoung. It hires an external firm for an annual audit under NM DFA rules; the FY2025 audited financial statements are dated January 20, 2026. Budgets are filed with the NM Department of Finance and Administration (Local Government Budget Management System) and adopted by council resolution each June. Transparency is formally good — budgets, audits and open-data pages exist — but the documents are posted as scanned PDFs that are hard for residents to use in practice.

## VIII. Policy Options & Strategic Recommendations

1) Publish a plain-language 'budget in brief' and machine-readable (searchable) ACFR and budget files so residents can actually use them. 2) Adopt and publish a formal fund-balance/reserve policy and a multi-year plan for deploying reserves toward documented community needs (water security, housing, behavioral health, youth). 3) Publish a Gross Receipts Tax sensitivity analysis showing how a regional-retail downturn would hit operations and GRT-backed debt service. 4) Report utility-fund transfers to the General Fund transparently so residents can see how much operating revenue comes from utility ratepayers. 5) Attach resident-equity safeguards and public revenue reporting to any automated speed-enforcement contract.

## X. Conclusion & Long-Term Outlook

Gallup is the rare small, low-income city that is financially strong — because it is really a regional commercial and utility enterprise serving a customer base several times its resident population. With $80M in governmental reserves and only $30.7M of debt, it faces no near-term solvency risk. The real five-to-ten-year questions are structural and ethical: the city's fortunes are tied to Gross Receipts Tax from tribal-area retail (a concentrated, external revenue base that also secures most of its debt), and its very large reserves sit beside a resident poverty rate near 27%. A downturn in regional retail would strain both the budget and GRT-backed bonds at once; absent that, the sharpest accountability issue is transparency and deployment — making the numbers genuinely usable to residents and showing how the reserves serve a community with such high need.
