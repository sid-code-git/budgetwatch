## I. Executive Summary

Little Falls, a small Mohawk Valley city of ~4,550, has been in the New York State Comptroller’s highest fiscal-stress category ("significant stress") for two straight years. In 2025 the Legislature passed a "fiscal recovery act" (Chapter 176) letting the city borrow up to $3.4 million to erase accumulated deficits in its general, water and golf funds. The city also failed to pay its 2023 and 2024 state pension invoices and is now on a repayment plan.

**Total Budget:** $11,376,924 all funds (2026 tentative); general fund $8,088,566 &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Per Capita:** ~$1,778 general fund / ~$2,500 all funds (pop. 4,550) &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Fund Balance:** Minimal — $0 appropriated fund balance in the 2026 budget; general fund held ~$929,000 of total cash mid-2025; accumulated deficits in general, water and golf funds prompted deficit bonding &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Debt Load:** ~$1.12M annual debt service; $3M deficit-financing BANs outstanding; up to $3.4M deficit bonds authorized (Chapter 176 of the Laws of 2025)

## IV. Debt, Pensions & Long-Term Obligations

**Bonded Debt:** ~$1.12M in outstanding annual debt payments; $3M in Bond Anticipation Notes issued for deficit financing across the general, water and golf funds; up to $3.4M deficit bonds authorized under Chapter 176 of the Laws of 2025.

**Unfunded Pension Liability:** The city did not pay its 2023 and 2024 NYSLRS retirement invoices and entered a payment plan requiring $360,000 in 2026 ($180,000 ERS + $180,000 PFRS). 2026 retirement appropriations total ~$1.3M. GAAP net pension liability figure: Not found in available sources.

**OPEB Liability:** Not found in available sources.

**Debt Service as % of General Fund:** General fund debt service $548,449 of $8,088,566 ≈ 6.8% of the general fund.

Chapter 176 of the Laws of 2025 (the "City of Little Falls fiscal recovery act") authorizes up to $3.4M in deficit bonds to liquidate deficits in the general, water and golf funds as of 12/31/2025. $3M in deficit-financing BANs are already outstanding. Because of this authorization, the Comptroller must now review the city’s tentative budget every year while the deficit obligations remain outstanding.

## V. Community Impact Analysis

### A. Education & Youth Programs

City budget does not fund schools; chronic deficits limit recreation and youth services.

### B. Facilities, Infrastructure & Parks

The city is leaning on water/sewer rate increases (15% water, 5% sewer proposed for Q2 2026), timber sales, and vacant-lot sales to shore up funds — signs of infrastructure and asset stress.

### C. Public Safety Staffing & Allocation

A paid firefighters’ union contract expired 12/31/2025; with no contingency budgeted and minimal reserves, public-safety staffing is exposed to settlement costs. Specific per-capita staffing vs. state average: Not found in available sources.

### D. Social Services & Senior Programs

A proposed $20/month ($240/year) garbage user fee on ~2,000 households would shift costs to residents; combined with rate hikes, this raises affordability concerns in a modest-income city.

## VII. Governance & Transparency Audit

Because of the deficit-financing authorization, the State Comptroller now independently reviews the city’s tentative budget annually. The FYE 2024 review found revenue overestimates and improper cost allocations across funds. Mayor Deborah Kaufman; City Treasurer Sarah George. The city’s own daily (Evening Times) merged into the regional Times Telegram in 2015; a volunteer hyperlocal digital site now provides some coverage.

**Budget document publicly available:** Yes — the tentative budget and the Comptroller’s review (Report B25-3-9, Nov. 20, 2025) are public, though the city’s own budget document is not prominently posted online. &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Last audit published:** OSC Budget Review B25-3-9, released November 20, 2025 (annual review required under Local Finance Law due to deficit financing). &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Public hearings held:** Common Council meetings; a public hearing on the proposed garbage user fee and tax-cap override was set for December 2, 2025.

## VIII. Policy Options & Strategic Recommendations

1) Adopt a structurally balanced budget that funds recurring costs with recurring revenues (per OSC). 2) Remove or de-risk overestimated/one-time revenues (ambulance, garbage fee, property and timber sales). 3) Bring NYSLRS pension payments current and hold to the payment plan. 4) Establish contingency appropriations in every fund ahead of union-contract settlements. 5) Correct fund cost allocations and stop misrecording water/sewer revenue in the general fund.

## X. Conclusion & Long-Term Outlook

Little Falls is borrowing to cover past operating deficits while leaning on uncertain new revenues — a pattern that, without structural balance, deepens long-term obligations. If it brings pension payments current, holds to the recovery plan, and budgets realistically, it can climb out over 3–5 years; if optimistic revenues fall short, it risks repeated deficit financing and continued "significant stress."
