## I. Executive Summary

Helena-West Helena, a shrinking Arkansas Delta city of ~8,900, is in a documented fiscal crisis. Its 2023 state audit carries a formal going-concern doubt, records deficit fund balances in three funds, and details $633,585 in unpaid payroll taxes, missed 2024 pension payments, and no IRS remittances since August 2023. The mayor who presided over the period was removed and arrested on felony tax charges in 2025; the governor appointed a successor in October 2025.

**Total Budget:** $8,052,769 (General Fund expenditures, 2023 actual) &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Per Capita:** ~$610 (GF revenues $5,448,751 / pop. 8,935) &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Fund Balance:** -$173,122 (General Fund deficit — below 0% of general fund) &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Debt Load:** $6,758,230 total long-term debt (bonds $5,445,000 + financed purchases $1,313,230)

## III. Revenue & Expenditure Ledger

### Where the Money Comes From

General Fund revenues $5,448,751; Street Fund $877,455 (state highway turnback + property taxes); sanitation/utility fees. Budgeted GF revenues exceeded actual by $388,020; Street Fund by $205,848. Loss of sales-tax revenue (now directed to a county jail) and expired COVID-19 relief funds cited as pressures.

### Where the Money Goes

General Fund expenditures $8,052,769; Street Fund $1,584,927; Landfill Fund overspent appropriations by $524,244; Street Fund by $424,531. Largest expense: payroll ~$140,000 every two weeks (~$3.6M/yr) across ~140 employees. GF accounts payable $1,036,692, incl. $633,585 payroll taxes, $140,000 county prisoner housing, $263,107 vendors.

## IV. Debt, Pensions & Long-Term Obligations

**Bonded Debt:** $5,445,000 bonds payable (1.75-4.75%, final maturity 2032). Legal debt limit $14,377,983; no property-tax-secured bond issues.

**Unfunded Pension Liability:** Not disclosed (regulatory-basis audit). City failed to make 2024 retirement-system payments, triggering loss of state general-revenue turnback.

**OPEB Liability:** Not disclosed (regulatory-basis audit).

**Debt Service as % of General Fund:** Not separately disclosed.

Total long-term debt $6,758,230 at 12/31/2023 (bonds $5,445,000 + financed purchases $1,313,230). Separately, the treasurer testified the city is over $2M in debt (past-due payables), up from ~$173,000 before January 2023. Landfill closure/postclosure backed by a $1,895,551 surety bond. No IRS remittances since August 2023.

## V. Community Impact Analysis

### A. Education & Youth Programs

City budget does not fund schools directly; fiscal instability limits youth and parks programming.

### B. Facilities, Infrastructure & Parks

Water-system failure left the city without water in January 2024; water rates were raised in response. Restricted sanitation fees were diverted to keep the water department operating.

### C. Public Safety Staffing & Allocation

Thin, imbalanced public safety: the mayor cited fewer than 10 police officers alongside ~30 firefighters; treasurer put police+fire staff near 50.

### D. Social Services & Senior Programs

Vendor repossessions, utility shut-off notices, and cash-on-delivery vendor terms signal service-delivery strain in a high-poverty community.

## VII. Governance & Transparency Audit

Audit found a repeat finding of a lack of adequate controls over accounting, plus unauthorized bank withdrawals and missing credit-card documentation. Mayor Christopher Franklin was removed by court order (effective July 1, 2025) and arrested on felony tax charges; Joseph Whitfield was appointed mayor in October 2025.

**Budget document publicly available:** No — the city does not post a current adopted budget online; the most recent authoritative financials are the 2023 state audit. &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Last audit published:** 2023 financial audit (Arkansas Legislative Audit), filed by the Legislative Joint Auditing Committee on July 11, 2025. &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Public hearings held:** City Council meetings streamed on the city YouTube channel; a specific budget-hearing notice was not found online.

## VIII. Policy Options & Strategic Recommendations

1) Adopt and publish a current-year balanced budget with monthly online financial reporting. 2) Negotiate an IRS payment plan and bring payroll-tax and pension remittances current. 3) Restore a formal inter-fund borrowing ledger; end diversion of restricted sanitation fees. 4) Pursue shared services with Phillips County (jail, sanitation, IT). 5) Re-baseline utility/sanitation rates to cost-of-service with a hardship tier.

## IX. Citizen Action Guide

1) Attend City Council budget meetings (agendas at hwh.ar.gov; streamed on YouTube). 2) File an Arkansas FOIA request with the City Clerk for the full 2023 audit and the payables ledger. 3) Track at each meeting whether pension and IRS remittances are current.

## X. Conclusion & Long-Term Outlook

Without a credible turnaround plan, Helena-West Helena faces continued population loss, rising past-due obligations (>$2M), and federal-tax exposure that compounds monthly. A new mayor, state scrutiny, and a possible ~$2M hospital sale offer a narrow path to stabilization — but only with published budgets, restored remittances, and enforced spending controls over the next 3-5 years.
